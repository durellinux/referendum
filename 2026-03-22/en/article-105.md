# Article 105

Appointments, assignments, transfers, performance evaluations, and the conferring of functions regarding magistrates belong to each High Council of the Judiciary, in accordance with the rules on the judicial organization.

Disciplinary jurisdiction regarding ordinary magistrates, both judging and prosecution, is attributed to the High Disciplinary Court.

The High Court is composed of fifteen judges: three appointed by the President of the Republic from among full professors of law at universities and lawyers with at least twenty years of practice; three drawn by lot by Parliament in joint session from among the subjects registered in the list referred to in Article 104, fourth paragraph; six drawn by lot from among the judging magistrates with at least twenty years of practice who perform or have performed legitimacy functions; three drawn by lot from among the prosecution magistrates with at least twenty years of practice who perform or have performed legitimacy functions.

The High Court elects its President from among the members appointed by the President of the Republic or from among those drawn by lot from the list compiled by Parliament.

The judges of the High Court hold office for four years and cannot be re-appointed or re-drawn by lot. The office of member of the High Court is incompatible with that of member of a High Council of the Judiciary. The incompatibilities referred to in Article 104, sixth paragraph, apply.

An appeal, including on the merits, against judgments issued by the High Court in the first instance is permitted before the High Court itself, which sits without the participation of the members who took part in the contested decision.

An appeal for cassation for violation of law is permitted against judgments issued in the appeal stage.
